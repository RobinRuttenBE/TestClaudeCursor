## Overview

Balloon sizing is the technique of inflating balloons to a precise, consistent diameter using a sizing template or sizing box. It is essential for any design that requires uniformity — columns, garlands, balloon walls, arches, and any multi-balloon structure. This tutorial also covers the "rounding" technique (squishing the balloon on a hard surface after inflation) to create a more spherical shape, and critically highlights that the order of sizing vs rounding affects the final result.

**Key selling points for balloon professionals:**
- The single biggest difference between amateur and professional balloon work is consistency
- A sizing box costs almost nothing but transforms the quality of every design
- Understanding the sizing-then-rounding vs rounding-then-sizing distinction prevents inconsistencies across a team
- Applies to every multi-balloon design — this is not optional for professional work

---

## Why Sizing Matters

- **Columns** require identical balloon sizes at every layer — uneven sizes create a wobbly, unprofessional column.
- **Garlands** look organic but still need controlled sizing for balanced visual flow.
- **Balloon walls** demand precise sizing — a single oversized or undersized balloon is immediately visible.
- **Any design with repeating clusters** depends on uniform sizing for a polished result.

Without sizing, even the best techniques look amateur. With sizing, even simple designs look professional.

---

## Equipment

### Sizing template / sizing box

A board or box with circular holes cut to specific diameters (e.g. 5", 7", 9", 11"). The balloon is placed on the hole — if it passes through without being pushed, it matches the size. If it doesn't pass through, it's too large and needs air released.

- Can be purchased commercially or made from MDF/cardboard
- Multiple holes allow sizing for different design requirements
- Should be a standard piece of equipment for every balloon professional

---

## The Sizing Process

### Basic method

1. **Inflate the balloon** using an air inflator — slightly over the target size.
2. **Place the balloon onto the required sizing hole** on your template.
3. **Gently allow air to escape** until the balloon passes through the hole on its own.
4. **Do not push the balloon through** — if you have to push, it's still too large. The balloon should drop through under its own weight/shape.

---

## Rounding Technique

Air-inflated 11" latex balloons (and other sizes) are often slightly pear-shaped rather than perfectly round after inflation. Rounding corrects this.

### How to round

1. **Place the inflated balloon on a hard, flat surface** (table, floor).
2. **Push or squish it down a few times** — press it flat and release, repeat.
3. This redistributes the air inside and forces the latex into a **more spherical shape**.

> Rounding is especially important for 11" balloons used in garlands, walls, and columns where the shape is visible from multiple angles.

---

## Critical: Order Matters (Size Then Round vs Round Then Size)

There are two approaches, and they produce **different results**:

### Method A: Size first, then round

1. Inflate → size through the hole → round on the table.
2. **Result:** The balloon becomes slightly wider after rounding — it will no longer pass through the same sizing hole.
3. Final balloon is **wider than the sizing hole diameter**.

### Method B: Round first, then size

1. Inflate → round on the table → size through the hole.
2. **Result:** The balloon matches the sizing hole exactly after rounding is already done.
3. Final balloon **matches the sizing hole diameter precisely**.

### Which is correct?

**Both are perfectly acceptable** — but you must be consistent. The critical rule:

> **Every person on your team must use the same method for the entire design.** Mixing methods within one project creates visible size inconsistencies.

Decide before starting: "We size then round" or "We round then size" — and stick with it.

---

## Tips

- **Never push a balloon through the sizing hole** — if it doesn't pass through freely, release more air
- **Pick one method and communicate it to your team** — the size-then-round vs round-then-size distinction is the most common source of inconsistency in team work
- **Squish firmly but not aggressively** — the goal is to redistribute air, not stress the latex
- **A sizing box is non-negotiable professional equipment** — it's cheap to make and transforms every design
- **Count pumps as a first pass, size as a second pass** — pump-counting gets you close; the sizing box gets you exact
- **Size every balloon, even when you think you've got the pump count dialled in** — latex varies slightly between balloons
