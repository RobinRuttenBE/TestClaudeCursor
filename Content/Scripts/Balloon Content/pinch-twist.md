## Overview

The pinch twist is a fundamental modelling balloon technique used to **change the direction of the balloon line** — essentially creating a corner or angle. It's a small bubble that gets pinched and twisted to lock in place, allowing the balloon to bend sharply rather than curve. This tutorial covers three variations: the standard pinch twist (between two bubbles), the double pinch twist, and the more advanced pinch twist at the start of the balloon.

**Key selling points for balloon professionals:**
- Core building block for virtually all modelling balloon figures and designs
- Once mastered, unlocks the ability to create corners, angles, and defined shapes
- The "at the beginning" variation is the skill gap between beginners and competent twisters
- Works with all Sempertex modelling balloon sizes (160, 260, 360)

---

## What Is a Pinch Twist

A pinch twist is a small bubble that is pinched outward and twisted multiple times to lock it in a fixed position. Its primary purpose is to **create a sharp change of direction** — turning a smooth curved balloon line into an angled corner.

Without pinch twists, modelling balloons can only produce curves. With them, you can create defined geometric shapes, ears, noses, limbs, and any design requiring sharp angles.

---

## Method 1: Standard Pinch Twist (Between Two Bubbles)

This is the easiest version — the pinch twist sits between two other bubbles that act as anchors.

### Steps

1. **Make a bubble** of your desired size.
2. **Make a smaller bubble** immediately after — this will become the pinch twist.
3. **Make a third bubble** after the small one.
4. **Close the two outer bubbles together like a sandwich** — fold them against each other with the small bubble in the middle.
5. **Pinch the small bubble** and pull it slightly outward, away from the sandwich.
6. **Twist 3–4 times** — not just once. Multiple twists lock it securely.
7. The pinch twist is now locked between the two outer bubbles.

> The sandwich position of the outer bubbles is what makes this version easy — they hold everything stable while you pinch and twist.

---

## Method 2: Double Pinch Twist

Two pinch twists placed one immediately after the other — used when you need two consecutive direction changes (e.g. going straight after two turns).

### Steps

1. **Complete the first pinch twist** using Method 1 (sandwich, pinch, twist 3–4 times).
2. **Immediately make another small bubble** after the first pinch twist.
3. **Close the surrounding bubbles together as a sandwich again.**
4. **Pinch the second small bubble** and twist 3–4 times.
5. You now have two pinch twists in sequence.

> A double pinch twist allows you to change direction and then return to a straight line — useful for creating defined features on figures.

---

## Method 3: Pinch Twist at the Beginning of the Balloon

This is the most difficult variation because there's no first bubble to form the sandwich — the pinch twist sits right at the knot end.

### Steps

1. **Inflate your modelling balloon** (260, 160, or 360).
2. **Make a small bubble at the very start** — right next to the knot.
3. **Pinch the knot with your fingers** — critically, grab the **knot itself, not the neck**. This is the key difference from the standard method.
4. **Use your hand/fingers as the substitute for the missing first bubble** — your hand acts as the "sandwich" that would normally be provided by an outer bubble.
5. **Pinch the small bubble, pull slightly outward, and twist.**
6. **The knot must end up underneath the pinch twist** — this is what locks it. The knot physically prevents the pinch twist from unravelling.

### Why this locks without extra wrapping
In the standard method, the sandwich of two bubbles holds the twist. At the beginning of the balloon, there's no sandwich — but the knot itself acts as a mechanical lock. As long as the knot sits under the pinch twist, it cannot come undone. No additional wrapping or knotting is needed.

## When to Use Each Method

| Situation | Method |
|---|---|
| Pinch twist in the middle of a figure (e.g. ears, joints) | Method 1: Standard |
| Two consecutive corners needed | Method 2: Double |
| Starting a figure with an immediate angle (e.g. nose, beak, foot) | Method 3: At the beginning |

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Only twisting once | Single twist doesn't lock — the bubble unravels | Always twist 3–4 times minimum |
| Grabbing the neck instead of the knot (Method 3) | The neck stretches and doesn't provide a lock | Pinch the actual knot — the hard lump, not the stretched latex |
| Not pulling the bubble outward before twisting | The twist has no room to lock properly | Pull the small bubble away from the line before you twist |
| Skipping the sandwich (Method 1) | Without folding the outer bubbles together, you have nothing to hold against | Always fold the two outer bubbles together first |

---

## Tips

- **3–4 twists minimum** — one twist will never hold. Build the habit of multiple twists every time
- **Knot, not neck** — for Method 3, this single detail determines success or failure
- **Works with all modelling balloon sizes** — 160, 260, and 360 all use the same pinch twist mechanics
- **The pinch twist is a direction tool** — think of it as creating corners. Plan where you need corners before you start twisting
- **Practice Method 3 specifically** — it's the technique that separates beginners from confident twisters

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Pinch twist | A small bubble that is pinched outward and twisted to create a sharp direction change in a modelling balloon |
| Bubble | A segment of inflated modelling balloon, separated by twists |
| Sandwich | The technique of folding two outer bubbles together to stabilise a pinch twist between them |
| Knot | The tied-off end of a balloon — in Method 3, the knot acts as the mechanical lock for the pinch twist |
| Neck | The stretched latex between the knot and the first inflated section — grabbing this instead of the knot is a common mistake |
| Direction change | The primary function of a pinch twist — creating an angle or corner in the balloon line |
| Modelling balloon | Any long, thin balloon designed for twisting (Sempertex 160, 260, or 360) |
| Lock | When a twist holds securely without unravelling — achieved through multiple twists (3–4) or the knot-under method |
