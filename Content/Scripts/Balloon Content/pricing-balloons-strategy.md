## The Pricing Formula

### Step 1: Calculate material cost (per item, not per pack)

Break every product down to its **per-unit cost**, including VAT (since most home-based decorators are not VAT registered).

**Method:**
- Take the pack price (including VAT and delivery)
- Divide by the number of items in the pack
- Round up to the nearest penny

**Example breakdown from the video (helium bouquet):**

| Item | Calculation | Unit cost |
|---|---|---|
| Sempertex 5" latex (×5 + spares) | Pack price ÷ pack quantity | ~£0.07 each |
| Sempertex 12" latex (+ 1 spare) | Pack price ÷ pack quantity | Calculated per balloon |
| Hi-float | Bottle price ÷ number of squirts per bottle | Cost per squirt |
| Helium (G20 cylinder) | Cylinder price ÷ number of balloons per cylinder | ~£1.00 per balloon |
| Foil bear | Individual item cost | As purchased |
| Foil clouds (×2) | Individual item cost | As purchased |
| Ribbon (5m used) | Roll price ÷ 250m × 5m | ~£0.01 per metre |
| Mini bows (×4) | Pack of 12 price ÷ 12 × 4 | Calculated per bow |
| 260s (for decoration) | Pack price ÷ pack quantity × number used | Calculated per balloon |
| Frilly weight (×1) | Individual item cost | As purchased |
| Oasis glue sheets | Pack price ÷ sheets × number used | Calculated per sheet |

**Total material cost for example bouquet: £15.47**

### Step 2: Add your hourly labour rate

This is where most decorators fail — they don't charge for their time at all.

**Recommended hourly rates:**

| Level | Hourly rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Absolute beginner (starting out) | £20/hour | Minimum starting point — better than nothing |
| Established decorator | £25–30/hour | Where most should aim |
| Experienced/premium | £30–35+/hour | Confidence and skill justify this |

**Critical: labour includes more than build time.** Factor in:
- Client communication (DMs, emails, calls)
- Planning and design decisions
- Ordering supplies
- Admin and invoicing
- Travel time (if delivering)

However, be realistic — don't try to bill every minute. As you build a portfolio and clients start ordering from your Instagram posts directly, the communication overhead per order drops significantly.

**Important: never share your hourly rate with clients.** This is an internal calculation tool, not a line item on a quote. You quote a final price, not an hourly breakdown.

### Step 3: Add overheads percentage

Overheads cover the cost of running your business beyond materials and labour.

| Situation | Recommended overhead % |
|---|---|
| Working from home | 10–15% |
| Renting a unit/workshop | Higher — calculate from actual costs |
| Running a shop | Higher — calculate from actual costs |

For home-based decorators, overheads include: electricity, heating, internet, phone, storage space, insurance, packaging, cleaning supplies, equipment wear, etc.

**If you've been in business a few years**, use your actual accounts to calculate your overhead percentage (total overheads ÷ total sales × 100).

### Step 4: Add profit margin

**Minimum: 30%. Do not go below 30%.**

Profit is not your wage — that's already covered by the labour rate. Profit is what keeps the business alive: it covers unexpected costs, enables investment in new stock/equipment, and provides a buffer for quiet periods.

### The formula in action

**Example bouquet at different settings:**

| Setting | Material | Labour | Overheads | Profit | Selling price |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| £20/hr, 10% overhead, 30% profit | £15.47 | £20/hr | 10% | 30% | ~£43 |
| £20/hr, 15% overhead, 30% profit | £15.47 | £20/hr | 15% | 30% | ~£45 |
| £30/hr, 15% overhead, 30% profit | £15.47 | £30/hr | 15% | 30% | ~£50 |
| £25/hr, 15% overhead, 30% profit | £15.47 | £25/hr | 15% | 30% | ~£47 |
| NO labour, 10% overhead, 30% profit | £15.47 | £0 | 10% | 30% | ~£25 |

**The £25 price is what most people would charge "off the top of their head."** That's the price with zero payment for their own time. It's working for free.

## The Mindset Problem

### Why decorators underprice

The #1 reason is **not** intentional undercutting — it's **lack of confidence.**

Common thought patterns:
- "I couldn't charge the same as [established decorator] — she's been doing it for years"
- "I'm new, so I need to charge less"
- "People in my area can't afford that"
- "I'm not as good as other decorators I follow online"

### Why these beliefs are wrong

#### "I can't charge that in my area"
Every area has people across the full spending spectrum. Your clients exist everywhere — rich mindset and poor mindset exist in every postcode. The job is to find the clients who value what you offer, not to lower your price to match the lowest common denominator.

#### "I'm new so I need to charge less"
A new coffee shop doesn't open and charge 50p per coffee because it's new. It charges market rate from day one. A new business is still a business. If you've been trained, you've invested in your skills, and you're delivering a professional product — charge accordingly.

#### "I'm not as good as the decorators I follow"
Your clients are **not following hundreds of balloon accounts.** They follow you because you're local and your work impressed them. To your client, you are the expert. They see your work and think "wow" — they're not comparing you to every decorator on Instagram.

### The guilt factor

Many new decorators (particularly women building businesses from home) feel **guilty receiving money.** This is a mindset issue, not a pricing issue. You wouldn't feel guilty collecting a salary from an employer — your business should pay you the same way.

### Confidence comes from action

You cannot build pricing confidence by thinking about it — you build it by **doing it.** The first time someone pays your full price, the feeling of validation proves that clients at your price point exist. Then you know: if one person paid it, others will too. And that client has friends with the same spending mindset.

## Client Management Strategies

### Always ask for the budget first

Before quoting, ask:
1. "What are you looking for?" (expectations)
2. "What's your budget?" (spending range)

This prevents you from spending time on a detailed quote that's completely misaligned with what the client wants to spend. Most clients don't know what balloons cost — their expectations are often unrealistically low. Managing this early saves everyone time.

### Lower the product, never the price

If a client says your quote is too expensive:
- **Do NOT** drop your price to match their budget.
- **DO** offer to adjust the product to fit their budget.

Example: "I can absolutely work within that budget. We could remove the helium, simplify the cloud accents, and use one foil instead of two — that would bring it to £X."

**What usually happens:** The client sees the stripped-down version, realises they want the full piece, and pays the original price anyway. Giving them the comparison makes the full version feel worth it.

### Don't compete on price

If someone tells you a competitor charges less — let them go to the competitor. Your response: "We're not the cheapest — if that's the priority, there are other options. But we guarantee quality, reliability, and a product that's built properly."

Competing on price is a race to the bottom that kills businesses. Competing on quality, service, and experience attracts the clients who keep you profitable.

### The repeat client effect

Premium clients grow over time. Real example from the video: a client spent £600 on a first birthday. By the fifth birthday, the budget exceeded £1,000 — because the client saw the impact, loved the reaction from guests, and wanted to go bigger each year.

**Why this happens:** When your balloon work makes the host look good (guests taking photos, complimenting the decor), the host's ego drives increased spending. They're not buying balloons — they're buying the feeling of hosting an impressive event.

## Industry Impact

### Underpricing hurts everyone

This isn't just about individual profit — it affects the entire balloon industry:
- When decorators work for free (or near-free), it sets a market expectation that balloons are cheap
- Experienced decorators lose business to unsustainable prices, which forces them out
- Educators stop sharing knowledge because their students undercut them
- The profession as a whole loses credibility and perceived value

### The "busy fool" trap

Would you rather:
- **Option A:** Make 6 bouquets at £25 each (£150 total, full day of stress, 6 clients to manage)
- **Option B:** Make 1 premium bouquet at £150 (£150 total, 2 hours of work, 1 dream client, rest of the day free)

Same revenue. Completely different quality of life and business sustainability. Option A burns people out. Option B builds a premium business with freedom.

## Key Pricing Rules (Quick Reference for Claude Code)

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Calculate per-unit cost | Never price from pack cost — divide everything down to individual item cost including VAT |
| Include ALL materials | Every balloon, every squirt of hi-float, every metre of ribbon — nothing is "free" |
| Charge for your time | Minimum £20/hour starting out, aim for £25–35/hour as confidence grows |
| Labour ≠ build time only | Include communication, planning, ordering, admin — not just the minutes inflating |
| Overheads: 10–15% (home-based) | Higher for unit/shop — calculate from actual accounts if available |
| Profit: minimum 30% | Non-negotiable floor — profit is not your wage, it's business survival |
| Never share hourly rate | Quote a final price only — the formula is internal |
| Lower product, not price | If budget is tight, strip features — don't discount |
| Ask for budget first | Before quoting, establish expectations and spending range |
| Don't compete on price | Compete on quality, service, reliability, and the wow factor |
| Confidence comes from action | The first "yes" at full price proves the market exists |

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Hourly rate | The internal labour cost per hour used to calculate pricing — never shared with clients |
| Overheads | Ongoing business costs beyond materials and labour: utilities, insurance, storage, equipment, etc. |
| Profit margin | Percentage added on top of costs — minimum 30% recommended. Covers business investment, buffer, and growth |
| Conversion rate | Percentage of enquiries that become paying clients — 20% is realistic for many balloon businesses |
| Ghosting | When a potential client stops responding after receiving a quote — normal and expected |
| Busy fool | Someone who is always working but never profitable — high volume, low margin, no sustainability |
| Money mindset | A person's psychological relationship with charging and receiving money — often the real barrier to proper pricing |
| Wow factor | The emotional impact of a balloon display on non-industry people — a key part of the value being sold |
| Dream client | A client who trusts you, pays full price, and returns with growing budgets — the target customer |
| Premium bouquet | A high-value, detailed balloon arrangement that commands top pricing — the alternative to volume-based work |
