## Overview

A short technique tutorial covering how to inflate 11" latex balloons into a more spherical shape rather than the default pear shape. Combines three small adjustments: nozzle positioning on the inflator, body-first inflation, and post-inflation rounding. The difference between a rounded and unrounded balloon is immediately visible side by side.

**Key selling points for balloon professionals:**
- Three simple adjustments that instantly improve the look of every design
- The difference is dramatic in a side-by-side comparison — great for content
- Takes no extra time once it becomes habit
- Applies to every single air-filled latex balloon you ever inflate

---

## The Three Adjustments

### 1. Nozzle positioning

- **Push the balloon neck far down over the inflator nozzle** — further than you normally would.
- This ensures air enters the **body of the balloon first**, not the neck.
- If the balloon sits loosely on the nozzle, air inflates the neck before the body — creating a longer, more pear-shaped result.

> The goal: air goes into the round body first. The neck stays uninflated for as long as possible.

### 2. Body-first inflation

- **Hold the balloon tightly on the nozzle** while inflating — don't let it slide up.
- **Push the balloon down** onto the nozzle as you inflate.
- The combination of deep nozzle placement and holding tight forces air into the body cavity, producing a rounder shape from the start.

### 3. Post-inflation rounding

- **After inflating and before tying:** pinch the neck tight on the underside.
- **Press the balloon against your body or a table** — push/squish it a few times.
- This redistributes the air from the neck area back into the body, further rounding the shape.
- **Tie the knot tight** — wrap around the balloon, bring it back up, and tie off snugly. A tight knot prevents air from creeping back into the neck.

## The Result

| | Unrounded | Rounded |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Pear-shaped / elongated | Spherical / ball-shaped |
| Neck | Visible inflation in neck area | Neck stays tight, air in body |
| Professional look | Casual / amateur | Polished / professional |
| Effort | Default | 3 seconds of extra technique |

The difference is immediately obvious when placed side by side — and once you see it, you can't unsee it in other people's work.

---

## Tips

- **Make this automatic** — these three steps should become muscle memory, not a conscious choice
- **Every balloon, every time** — the technique applies to every air-filled latex balloon, not just specific designs
- **Tight knots matter** — a loose knot allows air to migrate back into the neck, undoing the rounding
- **Use your body or the table** — whatever surface is available for the squish step
- **Combine with sizing** (see: `balloon-sizing.md`) — round first, then size through a template for precision work

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Rounding | The process of pressing/squishing an inflated balloon against a surface to redistribute air into a more spherical shape |
| Pear-shaped | The default elongated shape a balloon takes when air inflates the neck before the body |
| Body-first inflation | Technique of positioning the balloon deep on the nozzle so air enters the main cavity before the neck |
| Neck | The narrower section of a balloon between the knot and the inflated body — should stay uninflated for a round result |
| Nozzle positioning | How far the balloon is pushed onto the inflator — deeper = rounder result |
| Tight knot | A snug tie that prevents air from migrating back into the neck after rounding |
