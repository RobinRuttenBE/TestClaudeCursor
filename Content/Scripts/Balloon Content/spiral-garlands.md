## Overview

The spiral garland is a modern twist on the classic balloon column and garland, using **two different balloon sizes in alternating duplets** to create a visible spiral pattern as clusters wrap around a pole or cord. The technique produces a structured, elegant look with minimal balloons and fast execution time.

Two methods are covered: building on a **pole/stand** (column style) and building on a **cord** (hanging garland style). An optional finishing technique adds **link-o-loon chains** draped over the garland for extra elegance and dimension.

**Key selling points for balloon professionals:**
- Fast to build — 14–16 clusters per column
- Low balloon count = high profit margin
- Structured, polished look that suits corporate and elegant events
- Dismantlable and reusable — balloons can be taken apart and rebuilt in a different configuration
- Spiral pattern is visually striking but technically simple
- Link-o-loon draping adds a premium finishing touch with minimal extra effort
- Versatile: works as a column, wall-hung garland, or grab-and-go piece
- Pricing benchmark: approximately £65–75 per column (UK market, quick build)

### Sizing note
The 7.5" / 4.5" combination was tested against other ratios and found to produce the best visual spiral. Larger or smaller contrasts were less appealing. This is the recommended starting point.

## Method 1: Spiral Column (On a Pole/Stand)

### Creating the duplets

1. **Inflate one 12" balloon to 7.5"** (large) and one **12" balloon to 4.5"** (small) using a sizer.
2. **Tie them together as a duplet** — one large, one small.
3. Repeat to create all duplets needed (14–16 duplets for a standard column height).

### Building the column — the locking technique

This uses a specific locking method where each duplet interlocks with the previous one on the pole:

1. **Place the first duplet on the pole.** Wrap the large balloon one way, the small balloon the opposite way. Push down — they lock together.
2. **Add the next duplet.** Each time, the **large balloon acts as the guide** — always position it first, rotating approximately 90° from the previous large balloon.
3. **Wrap the large balloon one direction, the small balloon the opposite direction.** Push down firmly so the duplet locks against the one below.
4. **After each duplet:** check alignment. Wiggle and adjust until the large balloons form a clear spiral line with no visible gaps.
5. **Repeat** all the way up, always following the spiral path with the large balloon.

### Key tips
- **The large balloon is your eye** — always place it first and use it to maintain the spiral line.
- **Push down firmly** after each duplet. The locking only works if clusters are snug.
- **Wiggle into position** — minor adjustments after each lock ensure clean spiral lines.
- It's partly a **mental exercise** — you're simultaneously performing the locking technique while tracking the spiral position. Gets easier with practice.

### Finishing the column
- Top with a foil balloon, a large round, or a branded/logo balloon (ideal for corporate jobs).
- Mount on a standard column stand or base.

## Method 2: Spiral Garland (On Cord)

### What changes from the column method

Instead of duplets, this method uses **quads** (clusters of four — two large + two small balloons) threaded onto a cord.

### Creating the quads

1. **Make duplets** as before (one large, one small per duplet).
2. **Combine two duplets into a quad** — twist together so each quad contains two large and two small balloons.

### Building the garland on cord

1. **Use a thick cord** (blind cord style is ideal — easier on fingers than thin string).
2. **Tie the first quad to the cord.**
3. **Thread each subsequent quad onto the cord,** always using the **large balloon as the guide** — position it in the gap between the two large balloons of the previous cluster.
4. **Push down slightly** on each cluster and **pull the cord with moderate tension** — not too tight, not too loose.
5. **Wrap the cord once** around/through the cluster before adding the next.
6. **The spiral forms naturally** as you work, as long as you consistently track the large-balloon position.

### Key tips for cord garlands
- **Tension control is critical.** Too tight = clusters bunch up and distort. Too loose = gaps appear and the spiral breaks.
- **Lean against something** (table edge, wall) while working solo — the garland gets heavy and unwieldy.
- **This technique is harder solo** than the column method. A second pair of hands holding the cord taut helps significantly.
- **Minimum 15–16 clusters** to see the full spiral effect. Shorter garlands don't show the curve clearly enough.
- **Longer = more beautiful.** The spiral becomes more obvious and impressive with length.

### Hanging the garland
- Attach with **fishing line and magnets** (for wall mounting) or tie directly to a frame/structure.
- Use **three attachment points** — one in the middle, one on each end — for a balanced hang.

## Optional Finishing: Link-o-Loon Chain Draping

### What it adds
Chains of connected link-o-loons draped over or alongside the spiral garland add a layered, elegant finishing detail. The chains lap over each other and follow the curves of the garland for added dimension.

### Making link-o-loon chains

1. **Inflate Sempertex 6" Link-o-Loons to 4"** using a sizer.
2. **Create duplets:** tie two linkoons neck-to-neck.
3. **Connect duplets into a chain:** take the next duplet, wrap and tie its knots to the tail of the previous duplet. Repeat.
4. **Cut off all excess tails** for a clean finish.
5. **Keep everything tight** — loose connections create a messy chain.

### Linkoon sizing note
Link-o-loons are traditionally sized differently from rounds — they are measured through the **width** (horizontally), not top-to-bottom. For this application, the standard round-style sizing works fine. For deeper linkoon technique knowledge, refer to Denise (Sempertex Brand Ambassador) who is the specialist on linkoon sizing methods.

### Attaching chains to the garland

1. **Find a balloon neck** on the spiral garland where you want the chain to start.
2. **Tie the first linkoon of the chain directly to that neck.**
3. **Drape the chain along the garland,** letting it follow and lap over the spiral.
4. **Use two chains** for best effect: one longer, one shorter, overlapping each other.
5. **Layer front-to-back** — attach one chain slightly behind the garland, the second slightly in front for depth.

### Design tip
> Have an image in your head, but be prepared to adapt on the job. Sometimes you need to adjust placement once you see how it sits in the actual space.

## Reusability & Dismantling

A key advantage of the spiral garland technique: it is **fully dismantlable and reusable.**

- The locking technique (column method) can be **unlocked by simply unwinding** each duplet — no cutting or popping required.
- Recovered balloons can be **regrouped into quads** and rebuilt in a different configuration (e.g., column → cord garland).
- This makes the technique ideal for **rental/hire models** and for balloon professionals who want to repurpose stock between events.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Spiral garland | A column or garland built with two contrasting balloon sizes, where consistent placement creates a visible spiral pattern |
| Duplet | Two balloons tied together at the neck — here, one large and one small |
| Quad / Cluster | Four balloons (two duplets) combined — used for the cord garland method |
| Locking technique | A method of interlocking duplets on a pole by wrapping balloons in opposite directions and pushing down to secure |
| Link-o-Loon / Linkoon | A round balloon with a tail on both ends, designed for linking into chains |
| Chain | A connected series of link-o-loons tied neck-to-tail |
| Knot lamp / Column stand | A vertical pole on a weighted base used as the structure for building balloon columns |
| Sizer | A tool used to inflate balloons to a consistent, specific diameter |
| Cord garland | A garland built on a length of cord rather than a rigid pole — allows for flexible hanging and curved installations |
| Grab and go | A pre-made balloon product designed for quick sale or pickup without custom assembly on-site |
