# Balloon Curls (Curling 260s)

> **Source:** Tutorial video (general technique reference)
> **Difficulty:** Beginner
> **Category:** 260 shaping, decorative accents, curling technique
> **Products used:** Sempertex 260s, Sempertex 160s (for solo method), a cylindrical object (for assisted method)
> **Video reference:** Balloon curls

## Overview

Two methods for creating curly/spiral-shaped 260 balloons — one requiring a helper and a cylinder, the other a solo technique using a 160 inserted inside the 260. Curled 260s are a versatile decorative accent used on bouquets, centrepieces, garlands, and any design that benefits from flowing, organic movement.

**Key selling points for balloon professionals:**
- Adds instant movement and elegance to any design
- Two methods available — one for teams, one for working solo
- Very low material cost (one 260, or one 260 + one 160)
- Can be pre-made and stored for on-site use
- The solo method (160 inside 260) is a genuinely clever trick that impresses clients and peers

---

## Method 1: Cylinder Wrap (With a Helper)

The easiest method, but requires two people.

### Steps

1. **Have a helper hold the tip** of the uninflated 260.
2. **Find a cylindrical object** to wrap around — a pipe, tube, bottle, or any smooth cylinder.
3. **Wrap the uninflated 260 around the cylinder** in a spiral — ensure there are no folds or kinks in the balloon.
4. **Inflate at a steady pace** while the balloon stays wrapped around the cylinder.
5. **Inflate fully.** If the neck end is too tight to tie, release a small amount of air to create slack for tying.
6. **Tie off.**
7. **Remove from the cylinder** — the balloon retains the spiral shape.

### Adjusting the curl

- For a tighter curl at the tip: use your hands to orbit/shape the end into a tighter spiral after inflation.
- Wider wraps around the cylinder = looser curls. Tighter wraps = tighter curls.

---

## Method 2: 160 Inside 260 (Solo Technique)

This method allows you to create curls without a helper — the 160 acts as an internal spine that holds the curl shape.

### Steps

1. **Hold the 260 with the neck pointing up and the tail pointing down.**
2. **Insert the tail of a 160 through the neck of the 260** — push it all the way through so that the tail of the 160 comes out at the tail end of the 260.
3. **Align the tips** — find the tip of the 160 inside the 260 and **tie the two tails together** with a knot. The tails are now connected at one end.
4. **Inflate the 260** while holding onto the neck of the 160. The 160 stays uninflated inside the 260 — only the 260 inflates.
5. The 160 neck remains outside the 260 (at the inflation end).
6. **Release some air if needed** for tying slack, then tie off the 260.
7. **Tuck the knot at the tip inside** — it hides cleanly.
8. **Shape the curl** by hand — the internal 160 acts as a spine that holds whatever shape you create.

### Why this works

The uninflated 160 running through the centre of the inflated 260 acts as a tension cord. When you bend the 260 into a spiral, the 160 holds it in that position — creating a tighter, more defined curl than the cylinder method, and it holds its shape longer.

## Comparison

| Feature | Cylinder Wrap | 160 Inside 260 |
|---|---|---|
| People needed | 2 | 1 |
| Curl tightness | Moderate (depends on cylinder diameter) | Tight (controlled by hand shaping) |
| Shape retention | Good | Excellent (160 spine holds it) |
| Speed | Fast | Slightly slower (insertion step) |
| Best for | Batch production with a team | Solo work, precise curls |

## Tips

- **No folds when wrapping** — a fold creates a weak point that can pop during inflation or create an uneven curl
- **Inflate at a steady pace** — sudden bursts of air increase the risk of popping, especially when the balloon is wrapped tightly
- **Release air before tying if needed** — a fully inflated 260 can be difficult to tie; letting a small amount out gives you workable slack at the neck
- **Tuck the tip knot inside (Method 2)** — this hides the mechanics for a cleaner look
- **Pre-stretch both balloons** — especially for Method 2, a pre-stretched 160 slides through the 260 more easily
- **Cylinder diameter determines curl size** — use a narrow cylinder (marker pen) for tight ringlets, a wider one (bottle) for loose flowing curls

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Curl / spiral | A 260 balloon shaped into a corkscrew or spiral form |
| 260 | A long, thin modelling balloon (2" diameter, 60" length when fully inflated) — the standard balloon used for curls |
| 160 | A thinner modelling balloon (1" diameter, 60" length) — used as an internal spine in the solo curling method |
| Cylinder wrap | Wrapping an uninflated 260 around a cylindrical object before inflation to create a curl |
| Internal spine | The uninflated 160 running through the centre of an inflated 260, holding the curl shape in place |
| Neck | The uninflated stretch of latex at the inflation end of the balloon — used for tying |
| Tail | The closed tip end of a balloon (opposite the neck) |
| Shape retention | How well a curl holds its shape over time — the 160 spine method has superior retention |
