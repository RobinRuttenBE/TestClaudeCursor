## Overview

A realistic pumpkin shape built from six 18" balloons using a technique called "bean distortion" — a variation of the apple distortion where one side of the balloon is deliberately deflated and the attachment points are tied together, creating a triangular wedge shape. When six of these wedges are clustered together, they naturally fan open into a pumpkin form without any external support. Finished with a 360 tulip-twist stem and spiral 260 vines.

**Key selling points for balloon professionals:**
- Teaches three distinct techniques in one design (bean distortion, tulip twist, spiral curls)
- Highly realistic organic shape — doesn't look like "just balloons"
- Self-supporting structure — the bean shape naturally holds the pumpkin open
- Seasonal staple with strong commercial demand (Halloween, autumn events)
- Only 6 balloons for the main body — impressive result for minimal materials

## Bean Distortion: Step by Step

The bean distortion is the core technique. It's a variation of the apple distortion with an extra shaping step.

### Preparing the balloon

1. **Stretch the 18" balloon first** — fully inflate it with air, then fully deflate. This makes the latex soft and pliable, which is essential for the distortion.
2. **Inflate to approximately 10"** (on a machine: roughly 4 seconds — adjust to your equipment).
3. **Tie the knot close to the balloon's mouth** — leave a gap/space between the knot and the inflated body. Do NOT tie tight against the inflated part.
4. **Check softness** — the balloon must be very soft. This is critical for the distortion to work.

### Creating the apple distortion

1. **Take a Sempertex 260** (clear or matching colour) and tie the **middle** of the 260 to the neck of the 18" balloon. Both halves of the 260 should hang from the neck.
2. **Confirm the connection is secure** — it must not come undone under pressure.
3. **Push the knot of the 18" inward** (apple/nipple distortion) — push the tied end into the balloon body.
4. **While holding the pushed-in knot with your finger inside**, twist a couple of times.
5. **Use the second half of the 260** to tie around the twist point, locking the distortion in place.
6. **Tie a triple knot** for security.
7. **Cut the short excess piece of 260** — you no longer need it.

You now have an apple distortion with two 260 attachment tails.

### Converting to bean distortion

1. **Push the air to one side** of the balloon — one side inflated, one side deflated.
2. **Tie the two 260 attachment tails together** as tightly as possible, right at the middle of the balloon.
3. The result is a **bean/wedge/triangle shape** — one rounded inflated end and one pinched deflated end.

> The name "bean distortion" comes from the final shape resembling a bean. The triangular wedge profile is what allows six of them to fan into a pumpkin.

## Building the Pumpkin Body

### Assembly (6-balloon cluster)

1. **Prepare 6 bean-distorted 18" balloons** — all identical.
2. **Tie into duplets** (pairs of 2) — you need 3 duplets.
3. **Duplets must be extremely tight** — pull the 260 tails hard before tying. Strong pressure at the centre is essential.
4. **Twist the 3 duplets together** into a 6-balloon cluster — same technique as a standard round balloon cluster, just with bean shapes.
5. **The last 2 balloons** (completing the cluster from 4 to 6) require more force — use a table or your body for leverage.

### Why it self-supports

The triangular/wedge shape of each bean distortion means the narrow ends all point inward while the rounded ends fan outward. This creates a natural pumpkin shape that **stays open on its own** — no pressing, no support structure needed.

### The messy centre

The uninflated 260 tails will bunch up in the centre. **Do not cut them** — they're used to attach the top cluster.

## Top Green Cluster

### Making the cluster

1. **Use 6x Sempertex 5" balloons** in Forest Green.
2. **Inflate to a size that covers the centre mechanics** — exact size isn't critical. On a machine, approximately 0.4 seconds (adjust to your equipment).
3. **Squeeze each balloon slightly after inflating** to make them more rounded.
4. **Tie into 3 duplets**, then twist together into a 6-balloon cluster.

### Attaching to the pumpkin body

1. **Take two uninflated 260 tails from the centre** (using two together is stronger than one).
2. **Place the green cluster on top of the pumpkin**, over the messy centre.
3. **Wrap the doubled 260 tails around the green cluster** in figure-of-eight patterns, pulling tight.
4. This hides the mechanics and gives the pumpkin its characteristic green top.

### Bottom cover (optional)

- Place a Sempertex 5" balloon underneath to cover the bottom mechanics.
- This can also be a **water-filled weight balloon** — covers the bottom AND stabilises the pumpkin on a surface. Hidden from view.

## The Stem (360 Tulip Twist)

### Steps

1. **Use a Sempertex 360 in Coffee colour.**
2. **Inflate a small section** — just enough for the stem length.
3. **Perform a tulip twist** (same mechanics as an apple distortion, but on a modelling balloon):
   - Push the knot end deep into the inflated section with your finger.
   - Deform the shape slightly — this is desirable, it looks more natural.
   - Remove your finger and twist a couple of times to lock.
4. **Deflate the remaining uninflated portion** of the 360.
5. **Tie the stem** using the uninflated tail — wrap around a neck between the green cluster balloons.

> The tulip twist on a 360 creates a very natural-looking stem shape. A deep push creates a more bulbous, realistic stem.

## Spiral Vine Curls (260s)

### Preparing the curls

1. **Use 2x Sempertex 260 in Forest Green.**
2. **Stretch both first** — fully inflate, then fully deflate.
3. **Create the spiral shape before inflating:**
   - Hold two fingers like a "gun" shape.
   - Place the tip of the 260 between your fingers and lock with your thumb.
   - Wrap the 260 around your two fingers in a tight spiral.
4. **Inflate while still in the spiral shape** — the balloon will take on the curl.
5. **Cut to desired length** — you don't need the full balloon.
6. **Tie off.**

### Attaching the vines

1. **Tie both curled 260s together** with a simple knot.
2. **Leave a medium gap between them** — not too long, not too short. The gap is the attachment mechanism.
3. **Pass the gap between the green clusters** at the top of the pumpkin.
4. **Position the curls** to fall naturally on either side.

## Tips

- **Stretching is non-negotiable** — an unstretched balloon won't be soft enough for the distortion to work
- **Tie the knot close to the mouth, not the body** — the gap between knot and inflated part is needed for the distortion mechanics
- **Duplets must be extremely tight** — loose duplets = a pumpkin that doesn't hold its shape
- **Don't cut the centre 260 tails** — they're your attachment mechanism for the top cluster
- **Use two 260 tails together** when wrapping — double thickness is significantly stronger
- **Push deep for the tulip twist** — a shallow push looks artificial; a deep push looks organic
- **Wrap spirals on your fingers before inflating** — inflating first and then trying to curl doesn't produce the same result
- **The gap in the vine attachment is functional** — it passes between the clusters to hold the vines in position

## Key Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Apple distortion | Pushing the knot end of a round balloon inward and twisting to lock — creates an indented shape (also called nipple distortion) |
| Bean distortion | An apple distortion where the air is then pushed to one side and the attachment points tied together — creates a triangular wedge shape |
| Tulip twist | The same inward-push distortion applied to a modelling balloon (160/260/360) — used for stems, buds, and organic shapes |
| Spiral curl | A 260 wrapped around fingers before inflation to create a permanent curly/vine shape |
| Duplet | Two balloons tied together at the neck — the building block for clusters |
| 6-balloon cluster | Three duplets twisted together — the standard large cluster (used here for both the pumpkin body and the green top) |
| Triple knot | Three knots tied for extra security — recommended when locking distortions |
| Water weight | A small balloon filled with water, used as a hidden stabiliser underneath a display |
| 360 | A modelling balloon with tails on both ends (like a Link-o-Loon but larger diameter) — used here for the pumpkin stem |
| Neck | The uninflated stretch of latex between the knot and the inflated body of a balloon — used for tying and attaching |
