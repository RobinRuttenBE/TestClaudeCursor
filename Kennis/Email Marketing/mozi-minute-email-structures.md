# Mozi Minute Emails: Structural Reference for Email Writing

> **Purpose:** Structural and mechanical reference for Claude Code when writing marketing emails. These 5 emails from Alex Hormozi's "Mozi Minute" series demonstrate high-performing email patterns: hooks, pacing, storytelling, reframes, and CTA placement.
> **Usage:** Extract the STRUCTURE and MECHANICS — not the tone. Hormozi writes in a casual, bro-ish, direct American voice. STX EU emails should use the same structural patterns but in the STX EU tone: professional, warm, educational, community-focused, "from balloon artists, for balloon artists."

## Pattern Summary: What Makes These Emails Work

### Universal structure (all 5 emails follow this)

1. **Quote/epigraph** — One-line wisdom at the top (sets intellectual tone, primes the reader)
2. **Title hook** — Short, punchy, curiosity-driven (e.g., "The Agency Trap," "Your Market Isn't Saturated")
3. **Problem statement** — 1–3 sentences identifying a mistake the reader is probably making
4. **Stakes** — Why this mistake costs them money/time/freedom
5. **Framework/solution** — The actionable "how" — usually a numbered list, script, or step-by-step
6. **Real story** — A specific example (personal or client) proving the framework works
7. **Bottom line** — 1–2 sentence summary of the takeaway
8. **Signature line** — A pithy restatement of the core lesson (e.g., "Cut what they ignore," "Pay to learn")
9. **PS** — Humorous or emotional one-liner (often a meme reference or relatable feeling)
10. **CTA section** — 2–3 tiered offers (free → paid → premium) with short descriptions

### Key mechanical patterns

| Pattern | How it works | STX EU adaptation |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening quote** | Unattributed one-liner that frames the email's theme | Use a balloon industry insight, customer quote, or short provocative statement |
| **Problem-first hook** | Starts with what the reader is doing wrong — creates immediate relevance | Lead with a common decorator mistake or misconception |
| **"Most people" framing** | "Most entrepreneurs do X" — reader self-identifies without being attacked directly | "Most decorators do X" / "Most balloon businesses do X" |
| **Exact scripting** | Gives word-for-word scripts the reader can copy-paste | Give exact email templates, client conversation scripts, social media captions |
| **Real story as proof** | Personal anecdote with specific numbers/details — not hypothetical | Use STX EU campaign data, decorator success stories, or brand ambassador examples |
| **Reframe technique** | Takes a limiting belief and rewrites it as a skill gap | Reframe decorator complaints into actionable improvements |
| **Bottom line** | Ultra-short summary — forces clarity on the single takeaway | One sentence. Bold it. Make it the thing they remember. |
| **Tiered CTA** | Multiple ways to engage — free resource, mid-tier action, premium offer | Free: content/download. Mid: workshop/webinar. Premium: consultation/programme |
| **PS as personality** | The PS is funny, relatable, or emotionally resonant — never salesy | Keep it human — a relatable decorator moment, a joke, an emoji-worthy observation |

## Email 1: "The Agency Trap"

### Structure breakdown

**Hook type:** Contrarian advice (hiring agencies is actually bad — even when they're good)

**Pattern:** Problem → Why it fails → Exact script to fix it → Example → Bottom line

**Key structural elements:**
- Opens with a universal experience (hiring help that doesn't work)
- Uses a **timeline narrative** — shows how the problem unfolds step by step with parenthetical reactions: "(yay)" → "(ruh roh)" → "(womp womp)"
- Provides **exact word-for-word scripting** the reader can use immediately
- Explains **why the script works** from the other party's perspective (the agency agrees because...)
- Ends with a clear action framework: compare → beat → downgrade → cancel → reinvest

**STX EU application:**
- This "hire to learn, then internalise" framework applies to decorators hiring help (photographers, social media managers, assistants)
- The timeline narrative style (step 1 → step 2 → step 3 with emotional reactions) is excellent for showing common decorator journeys
- The exact-scripting approach works for client conversation templates

## Email 2: "Cost Cutting Hack"

### Structure breakdown

**Hook type:** Hidden money (you're already spending on things your customers don't want)

**Pattern:** Insight → Survey method (exact questions) → How to interpret results → Real story with numbers → Bottom line

**Key structural elements:**
- Introduces the **"Feature Survey"** — two questions with memorable names: "Last One Standing" and "First to Go"
- Uses bold question framing that makes the reader imagine answering it themselves
- Adds a **segmentation insight** — the same data reveals pricing tiers (bonus value)
- Real story with specific numbers: 16 features → cut technology division (35 people) → churn stayed the same → profit increased
- **"Meaning..."** transitions — connects each insight to its implication

**STX EU application:**
- The survey technique applies to any balloon business with multiple service offerings (bouquets, garlands, installations, delivery, setup)
- "What would your customers fight to keep vs. happily let go?" is a powerful email concept for decorators evaluating their service menu
- The segmentation insight (different customer types value different things) maps to STX EU's three customer profiles

## Email 3: "Your Market Isn't Saturated"

### Structure breakdown

**Hook type:** Belief-busting (the thing you think is the problem isn't the problem)

**Pattern:** Common excuse → Real story that disproves it → Reframe technique → More/Better/New framework → Hypothetical example → Bottom line

**Key structural elements:**
- **Reframe section** — takes three common complaints and rewrites each as a skill gap:
  - "Market too small" = "I don't know how to advertise beyond aware audiences"
  - "Leads cost too much" = "My offer isn't compelling enough"
  - "No good staff" = "I don't know how to recruit here"
- **More / Better / New** — a prioritised framework (do More first, then Better, then New as last resort)
- Each framework step gets a one-line tactical example
- The hypothetical example (roofing company) applies all three steps to one business — shows how they compound

**STX EU application:**
- "My area is too small for balloon work" is the EXACT same limiting belief decorators have — this reframe structure works perfectly
- More/Better/New maps directly to content strategy: post more → analyse top performers → try new platforms
- The reframe technique is a powerful email format: "What you say vs. what's actually true"
- Can be used for workshop marketing: "Think there aren't enough clients in your area? Here's what the data says."

## Email 4: "Fast Pass"

### Structure breakdown

**Hook type:** Easy money (you're leaving profit on the table by not offering this)

**Pattern:** Observation → How it works → Exact scripting → Pricing principle → Real story with extreme numbers → Bottom line

**Key structural elements:**
- **Exact customer-facing script** — word-for-word what to say when offering the upsell
- **"No harm" safety net** — positions the offer so rejection doesn't change anything (customer stays on original timeline)
- **Pricing principle:** "Charge based on value, not cost" — with a compelling example (forklift repair: $2K cost vs. $10K/day loss)
- The forklift story makes the principle visceral — the math is so obvious that the reader immediately applies it to their own business

**STX EU application:**
- Rush orders are the balloon industry's fast pass — decorators can charge premium for same-day or next-day turnaround
- The scripting format works: "Most clients with events this weekend value having their order prioritised, so we do offer a rush service for an extra £X. Would you like to do that?"
- "Charge based on value, not cost" directly reinforces the pricing education from the pricing knowledge file
- Event decorators lose money by not charging rush fees — this email framework makes the case

## Email 5: "Find Your Activation Points"

### Structure breakdown

**Hook type:** Data-driven insight (there's a hidden moment that determines if customers stay or leave)

**Pattern:** Misconception → Definition → Why it matters (Harvard stat) → How to find it (step-by-step) → Example → Pro tip → Bottom line

**Key structural elements:**
- **Third-party credibility** — cites Harvard Business Review stat (5% retention increase = 25–95% profit increase)
- **Step-by-step research method** — not a framework or principle, but a literal process to follow:
  1. Find churned customers
  2. Gather data
  3. Filter for 3+ months and highest spend
  4. Take top 20%
  5. Study demographics and behaviour
  6. Find common factors = activation points
- **Concrete example** with before/after: "Campaign live in 7 days" beats "invoice paid" as the real activation moment
- **Pro tip section** — bonus tactical advice after the main framework

**STX EU application:**
- Activation points for balloon businesses: when does a first-time buyer become a repeat client? (After seeing photos of their event? After a second order? After joining a community?)
- The research method can be simplified for decorators: "Look at your best repeat clients — what did they all have in common?"
- The Harvard stat is a powerful email hook for business-minded decorators
- For STX EU itself: what's the activation point for a new B2B customer? First order? First reorder? First workshop attendance?

## Structural Templates for Claude Code

### Template A: Problem → Script → Story (Emails 1, 4)
```
[Opening quote]
[Title: provocative/contrarian]

Most [audience] do [common mistake].
[1-2 sentences on why it fails]

Here's what to do instead:
[Exact script or step-by-step]

Why this works:
[2-3 sentences explaining the psychology]

Real story:
[Specific example with numbers]

Bottom line:
[One sentence takeaway]

[Sign-off catchphrase]
[PS — humour or emotion]
[Tiered CTA]
```

### Template B: Belief-Bust → Reframe → Framework (Email 3)
```
[Opening quote]
[Title: "[Common belief] is wrong"]

Most [audience] think [limiting belief].
[1-2 sentences on why they think this]
[Statement: they're wrong]

Real story:
[Specific example that disproves the belief]

Here's what to do instead:
[Reframe 1: "What you say" = "What's actually true"]
[Reframe 2]
[Reframe 3]

The solution: [Framework name]
[Step 1 with example]
[Step 2 with example]
[Step 3 with example]

Bottom line:
[One sentence takeaway]

[Sign-off]
[PS]
[CTA]
```

### Template C: Hidden Insight → Research Method → Data Proof (Emails 2, 5)
```
[Opening quote]
[Title: actionable noun phrase]

Most [audience] don't realise [hidden insight].
[Stakes: what this costs them]

Here's how to find out:
[Step-by-step method — numbered, specific, actionable]

What you'll discover:
[Interpretation guide — what the results mean]

Real story:
[Specific example with numbers showing the impact]

Bottom line:
[One sentence takeaway]

[Sign-off]
[PS]
[CTA]
```

## Tone Translation Guide: Hormozi → STX EU

| Hormozi tone | STX EU equivalent |
|---|---|
| "Most entrepreneurs" | "Most balloon professionals" / "Most decorators starting out" |
| Bro-ish parentheticals "(ruh roh)" | Warmer, community-feel asides — "sound familiar?" / "we've all been there" |
| American business examples (forklifts, roofing) | Balloon industry examples (rush orders, wedding clients, garland pricing) |
| "$10M revenue" scale | Relatable decorator scale (£500 weekend, £2K wedding booking, 20 orders/month) |
| "Tap here" CTA style | "See how →" / "Try it yourself →" / "Get the guide →" |
| Aggressive PS (meme energy) | Lighter PS — relatable decorator moment, a wink, a shared frustration |
| "- Alex" sign-off | "- The STX EU Team" or personal sign-off from Robin/brand ambassador |
| Unattributed opening quote | Can keep this — use balloon industry wisdom, customer insight, or provocative statement |

## Quick Reference: When to Use Which Template

| Email goal | Template | Example subject line |
|---|---|---|
| Teach a specific tactic with exact steps | A (Problem → Script → Story) | "The exact words I use when a client says 'too expensive'" |
| Challenge a limiting belief | B (Belief-Bust → Reframe → Framework) | "Your area isn't too small for balloon work" |
| Reveal a hidden business insight | C (Hidden Insight → Research → Data) | "The moment your one-time buyer becomes a repeat client" |
| Introduce an upsell/pricing strategy | A (Problem → Script → Story) | "The rush fee most decorators forget to charge" |
| Drive action on a specific behaviour | B or C | "Why your first 7 days with a new client decide everything" |
